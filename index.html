Overview

About the Game

Gosh Jam It is an online multiplayer wacky western first-person shooter where the guns jam sometimes. This leads to multiplayer maps which are designed completely differently compared to more traditionally competitive multiplayer shooters. Rather than pure mechanical skill, the jamming mechanic forces players to constantly adapt, creating chaotic and memorable moments.

Halfway through production, I was offered and accepted a role as co-manager in addition to my level design work. I felt confident in the project and wanted to take on more responsibility to help foster its growth and make the game as good as possible.

Map Design

Big Dylan's Dynamite Factory

I wanted a map concept that was wacky, non-competitive, and uniquely suited to this game — somewhere you could only do in a game like Gosh Jam It. That's how Big Dylan's Dynamite Factory was born. Dynamite from the old west had to come from somewhere, and this factory became both the setting and the mechanic-driver.

Design Philosophy

In a party game you still want some skill ceiling. I worked hard to make sure mechanics weren't too complicated, too overpowering, or too numerous for the player to keep track of — while still being genuinely fun and unexpected.

The map is built around a central strip focused on dynamite being created, packaged, and delivered. I added layers above and below, with sides that accentuate the dynamite mechanic. Big Dylan makes four types of explosives throughout his factory:

  • Throwable Dynamite — Can be picked up and thrown; explodes on hitting the ground
  • Ceiling Bombs — Drop from above and explode on impact
  • Keg Barrels — Can be pushed around and have a massive explosion radius when shot
  • Big Dylan Himself — Stood on a platform throwing dynamite and players — a completely unique and memorable encounter

Iteration

Playtesting & Version 2

During playtesting, we discovered that players felt like they were fighting the dynamite more than each other. So we reduced the amount of dynamite on the conveyor belts to keep the focus on player combat.

Polish is extremely important to me, so I always aim for a proper revision 2 before kitbashing. Key changes in V2:

  • Cut the packing room — it was too long and no one was actually fighting there
  • My boss cut Big Dylan from the map (not my decision) — so I pivoted: I placed throwable dynamite on his platform, letting the player become Big Dylan
  • Replaced ladders to the lower floor with stairs to reduce awkward navigation
  • General polish pass across all areas of the level

The Big Dylan Pivot

When the boss character was cut, instead of losing the concept entirely, I flipped it — now the player can stand on the platform and throw dynamite at everyone below. It preserved the most memorable idea in a new form.

Production

Co-Manager / Producer Role

Midway through development I was offered a position as general manager/producer. I took it because I wanted more responsibility and to actively improve the game at the production level. My responsibilities included:

  • Extensive playtesting and identifying areas for improvement
  • Ensuring milestones and deadlines were met on time
  • Scouting and subcontracting specialists for roles the team couldn't cover internally
  • Overall team management, morale, and direction

The Vision

Creation & Concept

21 Ways to Die is my sole creation and passion project. I wanted to design a full game for my portfolio that was focused on what I do best — design — while keeping programming and art manageable for a solo dev. Playing a lot of horror at the time, I kept thinking: what if the characters in Five Nights at Freddy's actually killed you on screen, and you weren't restricted to one area?

I took the design approach of ICO and design by subtraction — making a very short, focused game. Two non-negotiable pillars drove every decision:

  • First-person kills that aren't cutscenes and don't feel scripted
  • The freedom to walk around and explore an area

And from those constraints, 21 Ways to Die was born.

Game Overview

How It Plays

21 Ways to Die is a first-person horror game centered on brutal first-person kills with no camera cuts, and exploration of a mysterious facility. The best way to describe it: Pac-Man in first-person, as a horror game with real-time kills.

The game has about 15 levels in which you are placed in a randomly generated maze. Monsters hunt you down. You must flip switches to uncover the door to the next level. Some levels introduce new mechanics or new monsters.

Discovery as the Core Loop

At the end of every level you read a paper file containing information about the facility and the monsters. Late in the game you learn the monsters are actually a corrupted and mutated family — four normal people. Their kills, designs, and behaviors are all rooted in their personality, interests, and insecurities. The story is the breadcrumb trail that keeps you playing.

Despite the horror framing, 21 Ways to Die is really about discovery. You're uncovering the 21 ways to die, learning about the monsters, piecing together the facility's history, and following the story — all while navigating simple, focused maze gameplay.

Production

Concept to Reality

After months of design work, I began developing one of the characters and taught myself to model a decent-looking character in 3D. But I came to an important realization:

Design by Delegation

While I could learn everything and build this game entirely alone, it would be a waste of time trying to master skills other people have already mastered. To save time and create a better product, I very recently decided to hire people — but I remain the creator, director, and designer of the entire game.

I still direct and design every aspect of the game. The team helps execute the vision while I stay focused on what I do best.

The Vision

Creation & Concept

Glitch Skater was created based on my love of the Sonic series and particulary Sonic Unleashed and the other boost style sonic games. I loved the idea of the low skill floor but high skill celling games, where you start off rusty but get better with time. You learn the level, the mechanics, the character's moveset through time and practice and after a while you feel incredible. eation and passion project.

There was just one problem with the boost mechanic. You go fast, but it is a set speed so once you got used to it and learn the levels there wasn't much else to master. I wanted something a little more chaotic and dynamic and that is how Glitch Skater was born.
